## Final Project: The Lifting Wheelchair <br> <p style="color:rgb(48, 48, 255)"><font size="+2">My name is Alexander Barrameda, and here is my final project! Due to the medical background of my family, I have seen firsthand the dangers of making someone stand from their wheelchair in order to get into bed (in the case of Assisted Living/Hospitals). As a result, I wanted to create an easy solution to this problem: a lifting wheelchair. After doing some research, I found that normal lifting chairs were incredibly expensive, with some units being in the five figure range. Therefore, I used the Digital Fabrication process in order to create a solution to this problem. Here is a video demonstration of my project, as well as some accompanying photos of the finished result.</font></p> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <center> <video width="2560" height="1440" controls> <source src="./vid3.mp4" width="800" height="484" alt="WheelchairWorking" class="center" type="video/mp4"> </center> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg24.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image1" class="center"> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg25.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image2" class="center"> <br> <p style="color:rgb(48, 48, 255)"><font size="+2">Here's how my Lifting Wheelchair came to be. First, I started by making a notebook sketch of the mechanism that would lift up my model "wheelchair". After making the sketch, I used Microsoft Paint in order to make a rudimentary visualization of where the mechanism would go. From here on, this mechanism will be called the "legs" of my wheelchair, and the "leg" would be inserted into a custom-made "rail" system. Here is the sketch and the visualization:</font></p> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./projectsketchjpg.JPG" width="400" height="242" alt="image3" class="center"> <br> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./wheelchairsketchpng.png" width="400" height="242" alt="image4" class="center"> <br> <p style="color:rgb(48, 48, 255)"><font size="+2">After making my sketch and visualization, I started my final project by gathering the parts that I believed I would need. My original plan was to have four legs around the wheelchair (two on each side), and for this reason, I equipped myself with four stepper motors, four A4988 drivers, as well as a few protoboards. In hindsight, I overestimated the amount of time that I had, and I was forced to scale back to not only a breadboard rather than a protoboard, but I also had to use only two legs rather than four due to the print times. After gathering my supplies, I made a quick DXF file for the model wheelchair itself. Unfortunately, my computer blue-screened immediately after I exported my DXF, and I was unable to find the file afterwards. I included an image of my laser cut, which should also have the dimensions in the image itself. The parts are rather simple from a geometric standpoint, and holes were put into some of the pieces in order to fit metal rods through them. For my 3D prints, I created a modified version of a rail and leg that I had already created in a previous assignment, as I scaled up the leg and rail dramatically. Afterwards, I started printing two more legs (because, at the time, I had believed that I was going to make all four) and realized that I would not have enough print time to make all four legs. After verifying that my first rail design worked, I checked my second print (which printed two rails at once) and used those, as they were the same color and had less supports, making it easier for me to work with the rails.</font></p> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg3.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image11" class="center"> <br> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg9.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image12" class="center"> <br> <p style="color:rgb(48, 48, 255)"><font size="+2">After the 3D printing process, I glued two of the 3D printed squares onto the inside-portion of the rail, whilst also gluing a section of timing belt to these squares so that my stepper motors could move the rails back and forth. It's hard to explain through text, so the images should clarify any confusion. Afterwards, I also used hot glue and epoxy resin in order to stick my rail to my motor (so that the motors could move the legs up and down), and this was rather time consuming. During this process, my two front wheels also snapped off, so I had to re-structure the front of the wheelchair so that it would be more stable.</font></p> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg12.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image16" class="center"> <br> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg17.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image17" class="center"> <br> <style> .center { display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 50%; } </style> <img src="./jpg16.jpg" width="400" height="242" alt="image18" class="center"> <br> <p style="color:rgb(48, 48, 255)"><font size="+2">After successfully mounting the rails to the side of the wheelchair, I had to sort out my coding and wiring for my stepper motors. After soldering extra wire to the ground and power and adding a heatshrink, my external power was ready for my stepper motors. After a previous failure with my wiring of four stepper motors (image shown below, the ground and power were swapped and thankfully nothing was fried), I re-wired my stepper motors (now two rather than four) and tested them while they were mounted.
